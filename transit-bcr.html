What each benefit and cost line means

Model shape: every quantity below is computed per year over the project horizon and then discounted to a single present value (PV). The benefit–cost ratio is PV benefits ÷ PV net cost (net cost includes the MCPF surcharge); NPV is PV benefits − PV net cost.

Benefits (annual social value created, then summed as a PV):

  • CS — consumer surplus: the value of time saved by riders versus their next-best option (ridership × minutes saved × value of time). Usually the largest benefit. Falls if fares rise; also falls as trains get more crowded, because crowding lengthens the generalized (perceived) travel time that CS is built from.
  • Crowding — inside CS, not a separate line: the crowding penalty is already counted within consumer surplus. The "crowding embedded in CS" figure in the breakdown is just a diagnostic — how much surplus crowding is costing riders — not an extra deduction.
  • Congestion: road congestion avoided by the trips that would otherwise have been driven (diverted trips × trip miles × a per-mile congestion cost). Only diverted-car trips count.
  • Mohring: the “more riders → more service → shorter waits for everyone” effect. Valued as a fixed share of consumer surplus (default 18%).
  • Accident: crashes avoided by removing car trips from the road (diverted trips × miles × a per-mile external accident cost).
  • Emissions: CO₂ and local air pollution avoided by those same diverted car trips, escalated over time if a carbon-price growth rate is set.
  • Labor: a small labor-market gain — cheaper commuting slightly offsets the income-tax drag on working. Valued as a share of consumer surplus (default 5%).
  • Agglomeration: wider productivity gains from better job access. Applied as a % uplift on user benefits (CS + Mohring) only — not on the externality lines or labor.
  • Fare transfer: when you set a fare away from the preset's reference fare, part of the change in consumer surplus is just money moving between riders and the farebox (already counted, opposite sign, in operating revenue). That transfer isn't a real resource gain, so the agglomeration / Mohring / labour markups apply only to the time-and-access part of surplus, not to it. This is why cutting the fare below the point where ridership stops rising no longer keeps "improving" the NPV. At the reference fare the transfer is zero.

Costs (converted to present values over the horizon):

  • PV capital + renewals, net of residual: each asset class (civil works, track/systems, rolling stock, fare/IT) is capitalized when built and re-capitalized on schedule whenever it reaches the end of its useful life within the horizon. Assets with remaining useful life left at the end of the horizon contribute a residual value credit that reduces PV cost — see "Residual value" below.
  • PV operating subsidy: the present value of annual operating cost minus fare revenue, summed only over years where it is a net deficit (years with a surplus offset capital cost instead of adding to it).
  • PV MCPF (marginal cost of public funds): the present value of the extra economic loss from raising the subsidy through distortionary taxes — the shadow price above $1 per subsidy dollar, applied to the PV subsidy.
  • No depreciation line: capital is counted when it's spent and then discounted, so there's no separate depreciation charge.

Time & asset parameters (each shifts the PV calculation, not just one year):

  • Horizon: the number of years of costs and benefits included in the present-value sum (default 60). Longer horizons capture more mature-year benefit but also more renewal capex; residual value at the horizon's end offsets the truncation.
  • Discount rate: the annual rate used to convert future dollars to present value (default 4%). Higher rates shrink distant benefits faster, which tends to lower the BCR.
  • Ridership ramp: ridership does not jump to its full modeled level in year 1 — it starts at a fraction of the mature level (default 60%) and rises linearly to 100% over the ramp period (default 5 years), reflecting real opening-year ridership build-up.
  • Residual value: the credit applied at the end of the horizon for any asset class whose last-built tranche still has useful life remaining (e.g., civil works with an 80-year life inside a 60-year horizon). It is subtracted from PV capital cost, so a longer-lived asset mix or a shorter horizon raises the residual credit and improves the BCR.
