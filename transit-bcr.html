What each benefit and cost line means

Benefits (annual social value created):

  • CS — consumer surplus: the value of time saved by riders versus their next-best option (ridership × minutes saved × value of time). Usually the largest benefit. Falls if fares or crowding rise.
  • Congestion: road congestion avoided by the trips that would otherwise have been driven (diverted trips × trip miles × a per-mile congestion cost). Only diverted-car trips count.
  • Mohring: the “more riders → more service → shorter waits for everyone” effect. Valued here as a fixed share (18%) of consumer surplus.
  • Accident: crashes avoided by removing car trips from the road (diverted trips × miles × a per-mile external accident cost).
  • Emissions: CO₂ and local air pollution avoided by those same diverted car trips.
  • Labor: a small labor-market gain — cheaper commuting slightly offsets the income-tax drag on working. Valued at 5% of consumer surplus.
  • Crowding (negative): the discomfort cost when trains run above comfortable capacity — in-vehicle time “feels” longer. Shown as a deduction from benefits.
  • Agglomeration: wider productivity gains from better labor-market access, applied as a % uplift on all the direct benefits above.

Costs (annual public burden):

  • Annualized capital: construction cost spread over a 30-year asset life at 4% (a standard mortgage-style annuity).
  • Operating deficit / surplus offset: operating cost minus fare revenue. A deficit adds to cost; a surplus is used to offset capital.
  • MCPF deadweight: the extra economic loss from raising the subsidy through distortionary taxes (the shadow price above $1 per subsidy dollar).

The benefit–cost ratio is total benefits ÷ net cost (including the MCPF surcharge).
